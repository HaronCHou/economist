**为什么老板们需要警惕“黑暗模式”（暗箱操作）？**

2025年10月16日 

- 数字设计听起来似乎无害，但“黑暗模式”却并非如此。**它指的是那些诱导或操纵消费者做出他们原本不会做的选择的用户界面**。你肯定见过很多这样的例子。比如，拒绝优惠的按钮上写着：“是的，我不想错过世纪大优惠。”；“立即注册奖励”选项的字体比“我只想买我来这里要的东西”的选项大三倍，亮度也高得多。取消流程繁琐到让人忘记自己最初想做什么。还有那种暗示有300个人正在查看同一间酒店房间的信息。这些做法可能非常有效，至少在短期内如此。芝加哥大学法学院的杰米·卢古里和利奥尔·斯特拉希列维茨在2021年发表的一项研究中，通过对美国消费者样本进行调查，揭示了在推广免费试用服务时可能出现的“暗黑模式”。一些轻微的暗黑模式，例如在标有“接受并继续（推荐）”和“其他选项”的按钮之间进行选择，使得参与者继续操作的可能性是对照组（仅提供简单的“接受”或“拒绝”选项）的两倍以上。而一些更具攻击性的设计，例如强迫用户点击多个页面才能拒绝服务，则使接受率提高了四倍以上。这样的数据很难让老板们忽视。当然，并非所有的暗黑模式都带有恶意。有些设计选择可能暗藏玄机，而有些则合情合理：例如，了解酒店房间或门票的库存是否不足就很有用。某些操纵手段是商业的固有组成部分：如果说广告并非如H·G·威尔斯所言的“合法化的谎言”，那么它也绝非赤裸裸的真相。消费者自身也会区分不同程度的欺骗。宾夕法尼亚大学的香农·邓肯及其合作者最近发表的一篇论文研究了人们对一家援助组织广告的反应。他们发现，人们更反感的是人为的手段（例如，使用儿童演员来真实地展现某个村庄的贫困状况），而不是误导性的手法，例如刻意挑选一个异常贫困的真实儿童。暗黑模式可以不经思考地复制。据致力于修复暗黑模式的Fair Patterns公司联合创始人玛丽·波特尔称，问题设计通常是从标准模板中剪切粘贴而来。生成式人工智能工具可能会加剧这个问题，因为它们是在充斥着暗黑模式的互联网上进行训练的。但企业管理者有两个重要原因需要更加重视设计选择。首先是监管和诉讼的日益增多。从布鲁塞尔到加利福尼亚再到伦敦，各国立法者都在严厉打击“暗黑模式”。上个月，美国联邦贸易委员会与亚马逊达成创纪录的25亿美元和解协议。此前，该机构指控亚马逊操纵消费者注册其Prime会员服务，并设置重重障碍阻止用户取消订阅。（亚马逊表示，该和解协议并不代表其认罪。）今年6月，一家荷兰消费者协会对酒店预订平台Booking.com提起集体诉讼，指控其使用非法设计手段误导消费者。管理者避免使用“暗黑模式”的第二个原因是，它们会损害公司的长远利益。消费者反感任何试图操纵他们购买商品的恶劣手段：卢古里和斯特拉希列维茨的研究发现，咄咄逼人的营销技巧会让遇到它们的消费者感到反感。斯坦福大学的纳夫迪普·萨尼（Navdeep Sahni）是这项研究的作者之一，该研究随机抽取了超过100万欧洲媒体公司的客户，让他们体验不同类型的订阅服务。萨尼指出，让客户难以解除合同也可能适得其反。一些客户体验的是自动续订的促销试用，而另一些客户体验的是自动到期的试用。不出所料，自动续订的客户留存率更高。但这并非全部真相。例如，自动续订选项的初始接受率低了35%。由于意识到最终可能会花费超出预期的金额，许多人干脆放弃了这项服务。萨尼先生表示，自动取消订阅最终对企业更有利。这种“黑暗模式”会让消费者做出适得其反的行为，对企业也是如此。

### Deepseek总结


好的，这篇文章深入探讨了商业中所谓的“黑暗模式”，以下是核心内容的总结：

**一、核心概念：什么是“黑暗模式”？**

“黑暗模式”指的是通过用户界面设计，**诱导、欺骗或操纵用户做出他们原本不会做出的选择**。它听起来无害，实则是一种数字陷阱。

**常见的例子包括：**

* **情感绑架**：拒绝选项被写成“是的，我不想省钱”。
* **视觉误导**：同意的按钮醒目，拒绝的按钮又小又不起眼。
* **繁琐流程**：取消订阅或退订的步骤极其复杂，让用户中途放弃。
* **制造稀缺**：提示“有300人正在查看”，制造紧迫感。

**二、为什么老板们需要警惕“黑暗模式”？**

尽管“黑暗模式”短期内可能非常有效（研究表明其能将用户接受率提高两倍甚至四倍），但管理者必须警惕，主要有两个关键原因：

1. **法律与监管风险日益加剧**

   * 全球范围内，从欧盟到美国加州，立法机构都在严厉打击此类行为。
   * **案例**：亚马逊因被指控操纵用户注册和取消Prime服务，与美国联邦贸易委员会达成创纪录的**25亿美元和解**。Booking.com也因使用非法设计手段面临集体诉讼。
2. **损害企业的长期利益**

   * **引发消费者反感**：激进的操纵手段会让用户感到被欺骗，从而对品牌产生负面印象。
   * **适得其反**：研究证明，虽然让试用服务自动续订能提高短期留存率，但也会导致**初始注册率大幅下降**（例如低35%），因为许多用户害怕后续的麻烦和费用而直接放弃。相反，**让取消变得容易**（自动到期）从长远看可能对企业更有利。

**三、文章的最终结论**

“黑暗模式”是一种短视的商业策略。它虽然能带来**短暂的收益**，但随之而来的是巨大的**法律风险**和对品牌**长期的信任损害**。聪明的管理者应避免使用这些操纵性设计，转而选择透明、尊重用户的设计，这才能实现可持续的成功。

### Why bosses need to wake up to dark patterns

Of roach motels and countdown clocks October 16th 2025 Digital design sounds innocuous enough. Dark patterns, less so. That’s the term for user interfaces which nudge or manipulate consumers into making choices they otherwise wouldn’t. You will recognise many of them. The button to turn down an offer that says something like: “Yes, I would like to miss out on the bargain of the century.” The “Enroll now for rewards” option which is three times bigger and much brighter than the one saying, “I’d just like the thing I came here for.” The cancellation journey so byzantine that people forget what it was they were trying to do. The message that implies 300 people are looking at the same hotel room as you.Such practices can be very effective, at least in the short term. A study published in 2021 by Jamie Luguri and Lior Strahilevitz of the University of Chicago Law School exposed samples of American consumers to dark patterns when promoting a free trial of a service. Mild dark patterns, such as a choice between one button marked “Accept and continue (recommended)” and another marked “Other Options”, made participants more than twice as likely to proceed than members of a control group exposed to a simple “Accept” or “Decline” choice. Aggressive designs, such as forcing people to click through several screens in order to decline the service, more than quadrupled the acceptance rate. Numbers such as these are hard for bosses to dismiss. Dark patterns are not all sinister. Design choices can be shadowy in some lights and reasonable in others: it is useful to know whether inventories of hotel rooms or tickets are running low, for example. Some forms of manipulation are part and parcel of commerce: if advertising is not quite legitimised lying, as H.G. Wells supposedly called it, it is not the unvarnished truth either. Consumers themselves differentiate between levels of deception. A recent paper by Shannon Duncan of the University of Pennsylvania and her coauthors looked at how people reacted to adverts from an aid organisation. They found that people objected more to artificiality (using a child actor to accurately represent poverty in a village, say) than to misleading devices such as cherry-picking a real child who is atypically poor. Dark patterns can replicate without much thought. According to Marie Potel, a co-founder of Fair Patterns, a firm that helps to fix dark patterns, problematic designs are often cut and pasted from standard templates. Generative-AI tools may make this problem worse because they are trained on the dark-patterned internet. But bosses have two big reasons to pay more attention to design choices. The first is a rising tide of regulation and litigation. Lawmakers from Brussels to California to London have cracked down on dark patterns. Last month the Federal Trade Commission reached a record $2.5bn settlement with Amazon, after the agency alleged that the retailer had manipulatedconsumers into enrolling in its Prime subscription service and then made it unreasonably hard to cancel. (Amazon says the settlement does not imply any admission of guilt.) In June a Dutch consumer association launched a class-action lawsuit against Booking.com, a hotel-booking platform, accusing it of using unlawful design tactics to mislead customers. The second reason for managers to eschew dark patterns is that they can hurt their firms’ long-term interests. Consumers dislike egregious attempts to manipulate them into buying things: the study by Messrs Luguri and Strahilevitz found that aggressive techniques upset the consumers who encountered them. Making it hard for customers to extricate themselves from contracts can also backfire, according to Navdeep Sahni of Stanford University, one of the authors of a study that randomised the type of subscription offer shown to over 1m customers of a European media business. Some were offered promotional trials that auto-renewed into paid subscriptions, and others were offered trials that automatically expired. People on auto-renewal contracts stuck around in greater numbers, as you might expect. But that wasn’t the whole story. Initial take-up of the autorenewal option was 35% lower, for instance. Aware that they might ultimately spend more money than they wanted to, plenty of people avoided the product altogether. Auto-cancellation subscriptions ended up being better for the business, says Mr Sahni. Dark patterns can make consumers behave in self-defeating ways. They can do the same for firms.
