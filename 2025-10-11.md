 军方人士对**逐步升级冲突强度**的概念并不陌生。20世纪60年代的美国核战略家赫尔曼·卡恩（Herman Kahn）提出了不少于44个升级阶段。卡恩的升级阶梯较低，例如“庄严正式的声明”；最高阶段被称为“痉挛”，听起来就不妙。办公室冲突的升级也遵循类似的逻辑：不断威胁或造成更大的伤害，直到有人退让或被解雇。员工走向白领战争的最初阶段并没有明确的规则。但它们与卡恩在思考国家间冲突时提出的某些步骤有着明显的相似之处。

1. 分歧点。这可以是任何事情：资源争夺、战略争论，或是怀疑手机充电器不见了。但为了便于理解，假设你已经两次请另一个部门的同事写一份报告，却始终没有收到回复。你强烈怀疑他们认为这份报告完全是在浪费时间。

2. 正式语言。第一个升级步骤是使用更正式的语言再次提出请求。你在邮件开头使用“亲爱的”，而不是你惯用的“嗨”。你提前感谢他们的帮助，“提前”一词强调了目前为止进展甚微。你“期待收到报告”，这完全是谎言。你不再是友好地请求同事帮忙，而是变得疏远、像个大使；你甚至可以想象自己戴着绶带。

3. 怪异的正式语言。如果仍然没有回复，你突然开始使用一些你在现实生活中根本不会说的话。你说“关于”，然后又加一句“前面提到的”。“根据我上一封邮件”是最明显的麻烦预兆。没人会说“根据”，这跟说“请”或“做”一样老套。在办公室里，这种措辞无异于挑衅。你现在是在试探对方的底线。

4. 立场升级。你的对手也以夸张的礼貌回应，解释他们为何无需提交报告。你注意到“特此”和“有关”这两个词，意识到他们已经升级了，与你针锋相对。这时，你们中的一人或两人都抄送给了你的老板，让这封邮件越来越像狄更斯笔下的人物写的。严格来说，这不算升级，因为对手看不到。但这绝非儿戏。首先，没人真正了解密送（BCC）的运作机制，所以需要花费大量时间研究谁能看到密送收件人的回复。其次，这意味着一旦冲突爆发，就会有人为你出兵。

5. 武力展示。由于报告迟迟没有出现，而且你的邮件中充斥着越来越晦涩难懂的语言，是时候展现你造成真正破坏的决心了。你在下一封邮件中抄送给了自己的老板。这相当于办公室里的阅兵式，你部门主管的强大实力被展示出来，作为一种警告。你传递的信息再清楚不过了：你不是一个人，而是一个大国的代表。

6. 更大规模的武力展示。你的敌人做出了回应，你看到他们的老板也被抄送了。到目前为止，一切都在意料之中。但令人震惊的是，你发现他们的老板的老板也被抄送了。他们的“阅兵式”规模是你的两倍！你的对手深谙升级优势之道，事态已变得极其危险。你可以继续索要那份该死的报告，但原本局部的冲突正有演变成全面战争的危险。

7. 断绝关系。关于这份报告的争执陷入僵局。你的老板质问你为何需要它，而此时的你也已不知所措。你的对手显然不仅读过克劳塞维茨和孙子的著作，而且似乎还接受过系统的军事训练。你无法在不抄送首席执行官或向人力资源部正式投诉的情况下进一步升级事态，但这两种做法似乎都无济于事。你给对手发了一封私人邮件，表示对他们的不合作表示遗憾，并会转而寻求其他同事的帮助。他们赢得了这场战斗，但你并未彻底摆脱困境。冲突只是暂时搁置，并未结束。

### 核心观点

文章认为，办公室冲突的升级遵循一个清晰的、可预测的模式，就像军事冲突一样。**冲突双方通过一系列不断升级的“信号”和“行动”来展示决心、施加压力**，直到一方退让或冲突陷入僵局。

### 冲突的七个升级阶段

文章将白领冲突的升级过程具体化为以下七个阶段：

1. **分歧点：** 冲突的起因。可能源于资源争夺、战略分歧，或像“报告迟迟不交”这样具体的事情。
2. **正式语言：** 首次升级。沟通语气从随意（如“嗨”）变为正式（如“亲爱的”、“提前感谢”），像外交辞令，标志着友好关系的结束。
3. **怪异的正式语言：** 进一步试探。使用“特此”、“根据我上一封邮件”等日常生活中绝不会用的僵硬措辞，这是一种明显的挑衅行为。
4. **立场升级：** 对手回应。对方以同样夸张的礼貌予以回击，解释他们为何不合作。此时，**抄送老板** 成为关键步骤，将冲突公开化。
5. **武力展示：** 展示决心。你将**自己的老板**抄送进邮件链，这相当于办公室里的“阅兵式”，意在展示你背后的组织力量，发出警告。
6. **更大规模的武力展示：** 军备竞赛。对手回应，并抄送了**更高级别的老板**（如老板的老板）。冲突规模扩大，有演变成“全面战争”的危险。
7. **断绝关系：** 陷入僵局与暂时撤退。由于无法或不愿进一步升级（如惊动CEO），你选择暂时退出。通过一封私人邮件表示遗憾并寻求其他途径，但这只是暂时搁置冲突，并未真正解决。

### 文章的启示

这个比喻生动地揭示了：

* **办公室政治的仪式性：** 许多冲突行为（如怪异的邮件用语）更像是一种表演和信号传递，而非有效沟通。
* **升级的惯性：** 一旦进入升级循环，双方很容易被流程推着走，最终偏离了最初的目标（比如，那份“该死的报告”本身已不再重要）。
* **代价高昂：** 这种冲突会消耗大量时间、精力并损害工作关系，其结果往往是双输。

最终，文章以一种幽默且无奈的口吻指出，在这种白领“战争”中，通常没有真正的赢家，只有暂时的休战。

#### A short guide to white-collar warfare How office rows escalate October 9th 2025

Military types are familiar with the idea of gradually ratcheting up theintensity of a conflict. Herman Kahn, an American nuclear strategist of the1960s, identified no fewer than 44 rungs on the escalation ladder. The lowerrungs on Kahn’s ladder include things like “Solemn and FormalDeclarations”; the topmost is called “Spasm”, which doesn’t sound good atall.Escalation in the office follows a similar logic: more and more damage isthreatened or inflicted until someone backs down or suffers Termination.The initial rungs that workers climb towards white-collar warfare are notcodified. But they bear a clear resemblance to some of the steps that Kahnidentified when he thought about conflict among states.1. Point of Difference. This could be anything: a battle over resources, anargument over strategy or suspicion over a missing phone charger. But forthese purposes, imagine that you have twice asked a colleague in anotherdepartment to write a report on something, and not received an answer. Youstrongly suspect that they regard the report as a complete waste of their time.2. Formal Language. The first escalatory step is the use of more formallanguage to make your request again. You use the word “Dear” at the start ofyour email, instead of your customary “Hi”. You thank them in advance fortheir help, the “in advance” underlining how little has happened already. You“look forward to receiving the report”, which is a complete lie. You are nolonger making a friendly request of a colleague. You have become distant,ambassadorial; you can imagine yourself in a sash.3. Weirdly Formal Language. If no response is forthcoming, you suddenlystart using phrases that never pass your lips in real life. You say “with regardto” and lob in an “aforementioned”. “As per my last email” is the clearestsign of trouble ahead. No one ever speaks the words “as per”; it’s onlyslightly less archaic than saying “prithee” or “doth”. In the office, this kindof formulation is fighting talk. You are now testing the enemy’s willingnessto engage.4. Hardening of Positions. Your adversary responds with some exaggeratedpoliteness of their own, explaining why there is no need for them to producea report. You spot the words “hereby” and “appertaining”, and realise theyhave escalated to match you.At around this point, one or both of you blind-copies your boss into thisincreasingly Dickensian correspondence. This is not strictly speaking anescalation, because it is not visible to the adversary. But it is not a step totake lightly. For one thing, no one really knows how bcc works, so quite alot of time has to be spent researching who can see a reply from a bcc’edrecipient. For another, it means that forces are now being enlisted on yourbehalf if conflict does erupt.5. Show of Force. With no sign of the report, and increasingly baroquelanguage now peppering your correspondence, it’s time to show yourwillingness to cause real damage. You cc your own boss on your next email.This is the office equivalent of a military parade, the awesome might of yourdepartmental head displayed as a warning. The message you are sendingcould not be clearer: you’re not an individual but a representative of a greatpower.6. Even Greater Show of Force. Your enemy responds, and you can see thattheir boss has been cc’ed. So far, so predictable. But then, shockingly, younotice that their boss’s boss has also been copied. Their military parade istwice the size of yours! Your adversary understands the idea of escalationdominance, and the stakes have become dangerously high. You couldcontinue to ask for the damned report, but what was once a localised conflictis threatening to become a general war.7. Breaking Off Relations. The fight over the report has reached a stalemate.Your own boss is asking why you need it, and by this stage you cannot reallyremember yourself. Your opponent has clearly not only read von Clausewitzand Sun Tzu, but seems to have had extensive military training. You cannotescalate further without copying the chief executive into the email or makinga formal complaint to HR, but neither seems likely to end well. You send aprivate email to your opponent to say that you regret their lack of cooperation and will be asking another colleague for assistance instead. Theyhave won this battle but you have not climbed all the way down the ladder.The conflict is frozen, not over.
